I.
A first lens

Every series is trend + seasonality + noise.

STL pulls them apart. If the residual still oscillates, you missed a seasonality and every forecast you build inherits the miss. Look at the panels before you fit anything.

Observed ≈ Trend + Seasonal + Residual. The residual here looks like noise — no visible pattern. If you saw a wave in it, the period was wrong (try 30, or use MSTL for multiple seasonalities).

II.
A bake-off

Always run the trivial baseline first.

Naive (repeat the last value) and Seasonal Naive (repeat the same day last cycle) are the bar every fancier model must clear. If they win, ship them — cheaper, simpler, equally honest.

ModelWhat it doesMAE (£)RMSE (£)MAPEDecision
Naive · yesterdayRepeats yesterday's value3,3463,75829.3%floor
Seasonal Naive · last weekRepeats the value from 7 days ago2,4002,87320.7%baseline

On the real NorthStar 90-day holiday window, ETS-weekly is the most accurate — and the recursive ML booster actually trails both ETS and seasonal-naive, because a recursive forecast feeds its own errors back in over a long, hard window. The booster's real edge is one-step, daily-retrain forecasting (≈£1,400 MAE, 12% MAPE). Average across time-series cross-validation windows (an optional extension) and the three essentially tie. The lesson holds: never ship on a single split — always show the baseline row, and treat one bake-off as a starting point, not a verdict.

IV.
A checklist

Three checks before you ship a forecast.

Skip any and your forecast is a confident-sounding guess — the most expensive kind of mistake in planning.

1

Did you decompose?

Look at trend, seasonal, residual before fitting anything. A pattern in the residual is a missed seasonality — every model downstream will inherit it.

✕ "Straight to gradient boosting."✓ "STL first; residual is white noise."
2

Did the baseline lose?

Naive and seasonal naive must be on the table. If your ML can't beat them, ship the baseline — cheaper, simpler, equally honest.

✕ "GB hits MAPE 19%."✓ "ETS 19.3% vs SNaive 20.7% — marginal; ship the simpler one."
3

Judged on more than one split?

A single window can flatter or punish any model — the holiday window did both here. Never shuffle a forecast; quote a range, and where you can, average across time-series CV windows (optional extension).

✕ "One holiday split — ship the booster."✓ "ETS edges it here; across windows they tie."

V.
Check yourself

Nine questions from the field notebook.

Click to flip. Adapted from lesson.md's review questions.

▸ Click to revealSTL residual has a clear monthly oscillation. What does that mean?
STL only modelled the seasonality you specified in period. A leftover monthly wave means there's monthly seasonality you didn't capture. Re-run STL with a longer period (e.g. 30) or use MSTL for multiple seasonalities.
▸ Click to revealWhy does STL plot four panels, not three?
The fourth panel is the original series. STL plots: observed, trend, seasonal, residual — so you can verify trend + seasonal + residual ≈ observed.
▸ Click to revealShould you use STL on log-transformed data?
If the seasonality grows with the level (multiplicative), yes — log-transform first, decompose, then exponentiate. STL itself assumes additive components.
▸ Click to revealSeasonal Naive beats ETS on MAPE. What's happening?
The series is dominated by stable seasonality with weak trend. SNaive captures the seasonality perfectly by just looking back one cycle. ETS adds parameters that didn't help here. Sometimes the simple baseline wins — that is a feature.
▸ Click to revealETS converged poorly and gave a warning. Trust the forecast?
Sometimes. Warnings often mean the data is too short, too noisy, or has structural breaks. Either get more data, switch to the gradient-boosting lag-feature model (HistGradientBoosting, as in the notebook), or accept it if the held-out forecast is still good.
▸ Click to revealTrain RMSE £500, held-out RMSE £2000. Diagnosis?
Overfitting OR the test period is a different regime (e.g. holiday season). Inspect residuals: if systematically biased, the model isn't generalising. Try more regularisation, more lag features, or check the test period is genuinely different.
▸ Click to revealScale lag features before HistGradientBoosting?
No. Trees don't care about scale. (Neural networks do.) Skip scaling explicitly — don't forget it.
▸ Click to revealYou have lag_1, lag_7, lag_30. Add lag_2, lag_3?
Probably not. lag_1 already captures recent state; lag_2/3 are highly correlated with it. Stick with biologically meaningful lags: 1 (yesterday), 7 (last week), 30 (last month), 365 (last year).
